Abstract

This study uses a cross-lagged panel network model with a sample of 4,333 children (Mage = 11.06, SD = 0.73; 50.8% girls at T1) to examine the relationship between loneliness and depressive symptoms over a two-year period. The cross-sectional network shows that loneliness is strongly associated with sadness, perceived parental pressure, and boredom. The longitudinal network suggests: (1) a reciprocal link between loneliness and both sadness and parental pressure; (2) a forward effect of loneliness on anxiety and being busy; (3) the loneliness-reducing effect of prior happiness and loneliness-increasing effect of boredom. This research highlights the complex interplay of emotions in adolescents, emphasizing the need for targeted interventions to address the interrelated emotional and social issues associated with the risk of loneliness and depression.

Introduction Depression Depression is a prevalent mental disorder in the general population1 and notably among children and adolescents2. It can manifest in children as young as 33, though it is rare in preschoolers, with a 1.1% prevalence rate4. However, the frequency of depression significantly increases in the teenage years, especially after age 115, when it reaches a rate of 2.6%6, with about 20% of young people experiencing it by age 197. Note that approximately two-thirds of lifetime depression cases among adults emerge in the teenage years8, beginning typically between ages 11 and 14, making this phase crucial to understanding the origin of this disorder9.

Childhood depression is linked to many long-term negative outcomes. It affects academic performance and reduces opportunities for higher education and employment. It is also associated with negative emotions, low self-esteem, suicidal tendencies, substance abuse, and physical health issues, leading to financial costs10. Youth depression may stem from various causes11, and–in line with the interpersonal theory of depression12–one of its more important sources may be problems in interpersonal relationships, expressed in loneliness13.

Loneliness Loneliness has been described as "a sad or aching sense of isolation"14, and is characterized as a distressing emotional state arising from unmet intimacy needs and a disparity between expected and actual social relationships15. Research indicates that loneliness peaks among teenagers and older adults, with about 10% of school-age children experiencing it16. Like depression, loneliness has numerous adverse effects on teenagers. It affects school engagement, motivation, and outcomes, reduces well-being, and is linked to aggression, suicidal tendencies, substance use, problems with sleeping, various physical health risks, and a broad spectrum of mental disorders such as anxiety17. The interplay between loneliness and depression Since the 1970s, studies have linked loneliness to depression18. Recent meta-analyses reveal a moderate correlation between them in both the general population (r = 0.5019; r = 0.4920) and children/adolescents (r = 0.4821; r = 0.6122). While these two experiences might share common causes and features23, factor analyses indicate that they are distinct constructs24.

Unfortunately, there is still a lack of clarity about which symptoms of depression are most strongly associated with loneliness. This lack of clarity can be attributed in part to the dominant perspective in the understanding psychopathological disorders, which is the latent perspective. A latent approach implicitly assumes that mental disorders such as depression cause feelings of sadness or worthlessness in the same way that the smallpox virus causes pustules, fever, and headache25. From a latent modeling perspective, the causes and consequences related to the underlying latent variable itself are more important than specific symptoms. In practice, from a latent perspective, constructs (such as depression) are aggregate measures (most often formed as the sums/means of the items that form a given scale).

In contrast, the psychometric network perspective sees mental disorders as systems in which symptoms interact and often amplify each other26. Symptoms are not merely outcomes of a latent cause but play an active role; they influence and trigger each other rather than stemming from a singular origin27. This viewpoint suggests that mental disorders emerge from intricate symptom interactions rather than a singular underlying (latent) cause28. Recognizing the varying importance of individual symptoms can provide insight into their clinical relevance and provide a more nuanced understanding of the etiology of mental disorders29.

Results and limitations of previous research on depressive symptoms networks and loneliness among teenagers Considering the significance of depression and loneliness in the development of teenagers, it is notable how infrequently the network relationships between them have been studied. Only sixteen studies from thirteen articles have addressed this topic (see Table S1 in Supplementary Materials).

These studies consistently highlight loneliness as a key component of the depressive symptoms network, often ranking it as the first or second most central element, underscoring its strong association with teenage depression and its potential as a risk factor. Moreover, they show that loneliness is closely tied to crying, feelings of sadness, worthlessness, unhappiness, and suicidal ideation. As a result, they provide evidence that loneliness is linked to depression, primarily via a negative cognitive and emotional style that triggers a sad or depressed mood30, a core symptom of depression31. This insight greatly improves our understanding of the relationship between loneliness and depression. Page 3/23

Page 4

At the same time, all these network analyses have been cross-sectional, depicting associations among symptoms at a single point in time, which precludes the making of claims32 about the direction of the relationships Thus, there are no solid empirical grounds for identifying whether an increase in loneliness leads to more sadness, or whether sadness contributes to an increase in loneliness. Consequently, although cross-sectional research can identify loneliness as a significant element in the network of symptoms of depression and its associations with specific symptoms at a given time, it does not offer a solid foundation for hypothesizing causal relationships.

Since cross-sectional data represent only one moment in time, they do not meet the "causality" assumption that causes must precede effects33. Given this, cross-sectional analysis relies on theoretical inferences to make arguments about causality34. A partial remedy to such threats to internal validity can be provided by employing longitudinal designs and data analysis with methods such as cross-lagged panel modelling (CLPM)35,36, which is often considered to be the most appropriate approach to the study causality when compared to other longitudinal models in non-experimental data37.

Unfortunately, traditional CLPM is not well-suited to the analysis of networks of symptoms. Nevertheless, the recently introduced cross-lagged panel network model (CLPN) offers a solution by combining network methodology with the cross-lagged panel model approach38. The CLPN approach allows for the analysis of the ways in which mutual relationships between constructs evolve, by adjusting for previous levels of each variable and estimating the autoregressive and cross-lagged effects. In contrast to the undirected networks derived from cross-sectional data, CLPN is a set of directed paths from one measurement occasion to the next. The interpretation of these directed paths is subject to the same constraints as those of the traditional CLPM38.

Current Study This study focuses on investigating longitudinal development in the relationship between loneliness and depressive symptoms, using network analysis modeling based on data from a large longitudinal study. The emphasis is on understanding the dynamic, evolving interactions over time (cross-lagged panel network) between loneliness and depressive symptoms, covering three general categories of DSM-IV symptoms of depression: mood, a state of diminished interest and fatigue, and feelings of worthlessness. Understanding the complexity of the connections between these symptoms is of both theoretical and practical significance. Knowledge of the prospective effects between loneliness and depressive symptoms can lead to the development of more effective and accurately targeted intervention strategies in this critical developmental stage.

Methods Data source and sample Page 4/23

Page 5

We studied data from the NLSY79 Children and Young Adults survey, conducted by the U.S. Department of Labor. The original NLSY79 started in 1979 with about 6,300 young women aged 14–21. From 1986, their children aged 10–14 participated in a biennial survey (Child Self-Administered Supplement–CSAS). Starting in 1996, this survey included a depression severity scale. In 1994, children aged 15 and older answered a more detailed questionnaire with a different depression scale (7-item CES-D).

Our study focused on children aged 10–12, as they were the only ones to take the same depression questionnaire twice, given the survey's biennial nature and the different questionnaires completed by older teens. We included only their first participation to ensure each child was represented once in the longitudinal analysis. For our sample, we included everyone who participated in the first two rounds (1992→1994). We removed those in the 1994→1996 wave who had also participated in the 1992→1994 wave, and followed a similar procedure for subsequent waves.

Ultimately, we analyzed data from 4,333 children aged 10–12 at T1 (Mage = 11.06, SD = 0.73; 50.8% girls), including 28.3% African American, 20.5% Hispanic, and 51.2% others.

Measures Children answered eight items representing the degree to which they had experienced depression. This self-administered supplement of the NLSY–taken from the National Commission on Children 1990 Survey of Parents and Children39–asked children how often they felt (a) lonely, (b) sad and blue, (c) nervous, tense or on edge, (d) happy, (e) bored,, (f) tired or worn-out, (g) excited about something they are looking forward to, (h) too busy to get everything done, and (i) pressured by their mother or father. Participants used a 3-point response scale ranging from 'hardly ever' to 'often' (see: Table S2 in Supplementary Materials).

Statistical analyses Item redundancy analysis The main analyses were preceded by preliminary ones in order to identify redundant items. A key assumption of network psychometrics is that network elements (nodes) are unique causal components, meaning that nodes are not exchangeable with other nodes of the network40. As a result, these elements should be clearly distinct, rather than redundant. Overlapping items can misrepresent relationships between nodes (symptoms) and affect the bias of various network measures, e.g., centrality41.

Redundant pairs of items were evaluated using Unique Variable Analysis (UVA)42. This procedure identifies local dependence using network modeling and a graph theory measure called weighted topological overlap (wTO). wTO quantifies the 'overlap' of items in a network by computing the similarity of their connections (number, strength, and sign). The redundant pairs of items were identified as those Page 5/23

Page 6

in which the wTO exceeded-as suggested in simulation studies–the cutoff value of 0.2542. The UVA approaches were implemented using the EGAnet package in R.

Longitudinal network (cross-lagged panel network–CLPN) In the main part of the analysis, the relationships between loneliness and depressive feelings over time was examined using a cross-lagged panel network (CLPN)38. A CLPN's primary characteristic is that it models relationships between items over time as directed paths. These paths represent the shared variance between a variable at one time point and another (or the same) variable at the next, factoring in all other variables at the initial time point. Let us emphasize that, contrary to undirected networks in cross-sectional data, CLPN paths move from one measurement occasion to a subsequent one38.

To minimize the chances of false edges in the network models, least absolute shrinkage and selection operator (LASSO) regularization was used. This adjustment to regression coefficients helps prevent overfitting and simplifies the network structure. Therefore, this approach decreases the quantity of nonzero regression coefficients (edges). The LASSO regularized regression and 10-fold cross-validation were conducted using the glmnet package in R 43. The analyses used lambda penalization value (lambda$min), thereby minimizing the mean cross-validated error.

In CLPN the edge weights can be interpreted similarly to a regression coefficient in the traditional crosslagged model (CLPM). As in CLPM, in CLPN autoregressive (also referred to as stability effect, inertia, tendency to not move) and cross-lagged effects are estimated.

The autoregressive effect refers to the coefficient in which a node at a given time point predicts its own value at the next time point, taking into account all other nodes at that time. In other words, information is provided on the rank-order stability of the constructs (i.e., stability of interindividual differences) from one occasion to the next.

Cross-lagged effects indicate whether and how strongly nodes in an initial assessment (T1) predict the values of other nodes at the subsequent stage of assessment (T2), while accounting for all other variables at the starting assessment (T1) and autoregressive effects for the outcome variable. Finding a non-zero cross-lagged coefficient (predictive relations over time) may suggest (though not prove) the presence of causal relationships38. For example, a positively cross-lagged coefficient between two constructs (loneliness and sadness) will imply that, when respondents have a high position on the loneliness scale (relative to other respondents), they will experience a subsequent rank-order increase in sadness compared to individuals with high loneliness. Therefore, in CLPN depression symptoms and their correlates can be represented as dynamic with interactive indicators, such that, for instance, previously low self-esteem leads to a later increase in loneliness, and previous loneliness fosters a later higher intensity of sadness.

In addition to autoregression coefficients and cross-lagged effects, strength centrality was estimated, which is the sum of the absolute values of the cross-lagged effects. This consists of in-strength (in-Page 6/23

Page 7

prediction) and out-strength (out-prediction). The in-strength indicates the relative prospective predictability of the target node and is calculated as the sum of the absolute values of incoming (crosslagged) edges associated with one symptom. The out-strength highlights its relative influence over time and is calculated as the sum of the absolute values of outgoing edges associated with one symptom. Note that the autoregressive path is omitted when computing outgoing and incoming strengths.

Results The severity of the symptoms between T1 and T2 (over two years) remained stable (Table S2 in Supplementary Materials 1). Small, statistically significant differences (Cohen's d < 0.07) were observed for 'sad', 'bored', 'lonely', and 'pressured'. The intensity decreased for 'sad', 'lonely', and 'pressured', but increased for 'bored'.

Spearman correlations for the instruments administered to the study participants are presented in Table S3 in Supplementary Materials. These coefficients range from − 0.28 to 0.39. The average of the absolute values of the correlation coefficients was 0.13 (SD = 0.08). Out of the 153 coefficients, only 13 were not statistically significant (p > 0.05).

Redundancy analysis Initially, we examined redundancies between item pairs based on the similarity between their connections with other nodes (variables) through Unique Variable Analysis (UVA). Using weighted topological overlap (wTO), we identified both in T1 and in T2 redundancies between the same two items: 'happy' and 'excited' (wTOT1 = 0.322; wTOT2 = 0.405). Given their similar characteristics and content, in subsequent analyses both positions were combined into a composite item using averaged scores, which will be called 'happiness' in the rest of the article. As a result, the final network was comprised of 8 items.

Discussion The purpose of our study was to examine the reciprocal associations between loneliness and depressive symptoms in late childhood, using longitudinal studies and a cross-lagged panel network model. We focused on how these elements interact over time. As a result, the study sheds light on the complex interplay between loneliness and depressive symptoms, including (1) various depressive moods such as sadness, happiness, and nervousness (irritable mood), (2) feelings of worthlessness driven by parental pressure, and (3) a state of diminished interest and fatigue, characterized by boredom or excessive activity. Each of these elements impacts the well-being of adolescents, with loneliness playing a key role as both an influence and a manifestation of these varied emotional states.

Sadness Our study reveals a bidirectional relationship between loneliness and sadness, a core symptom of depressive mood. Sadness is a complex natural emotional state characterized by feelings of unhappiness, often arising from everyday setbacks and disappointments or from a sense of loss in life Page 8/23

Page 9

related to relationship problems and difficulties in social interaction44,45. Therefore, the prospective relationship between loneliness and sadness is unsurprising.

However, the prospective effect of sadness on loneliness is less obvious and requires further exploration. In attempting to explain this relationship, there are two main pathways to consider. First, sadness might be connected to loneliness through its negative effect on peer status46. There is relative agreement that the expression of (positive or negative) emotion has a communicative function47, affects beliefs about the expresser48, and can be an important predictor of peer status49. The expression of negative affect has been found to negatively correlate with social competence and peer group status50.

This is also true for sadness. Sad people are often seen as passive and presenting behavioral inhibitions51 and less socially competent52, which lowers their attractiveness as an interaction partner and may increase loneliness.

Second, sadness may increase loneliness by negatively affecting one's interpretation and experience of relationships with others, irrespective of the actual state of those relationships. This phenomenon, which we refer to as the 'extended sadness' hypothesis, finds confirmation in research showing that people experiencing high levels of sadness tend to generate negatively biased ratings of their social functioning compared to ratings generated by observers53. Additionally, although sadness is primarily associated with a social context, it can also be triggered by non-social factors, such as loss of good health54.

Therefore, it may be hypothesized that "non-social" sadness gradually casts a shadow over the perception of interpersonal relationships, thereby intensifying loneliness. This phenomenon aligns with Beck's cognitive theory55, which suggests that negative self-perception can shape one's view of relationships. In this way, sadness reinforces feelings of loneliness by distorting both behavior and perception, creating a cycle of withdrawal. While our study highlights these pathways, more research is needed to fully understand the complex interactions between these constructs of interest. Happiness Our analysis also found a longitudinal association between happiness and loneliness, indicating that higher levels of happiness may decrease feelings of loneliness over time. Prior research suggests that happier individuals tend to be more extroverted, agreeable, and less prone to neuroticism56. Additionally, they are generally less self-focused and more compassionate, forgiving, trusting, energetic, decisive, creative, sociable, and helpful57, which often leads to stronger and more intimate interpersonal connections58. Consequently, happiness may reduce loneliness by fostering prosocial qualities and interpersonal skills that support lasting, fulfilling relationships.

Interestingly, our results show that loneliness did not predict a decline in happiness, in contrast to the dynamics observed for sadness. This is an unexpected finding, taking into account that loneliness is associated with an unmet need for belonging59, which is essential to experiencing happiness58,60. On the other hand, this prospective effect of loneliness on sadness but not happiness is confirmed by the theory of emotional complexity61, according to which positive and negative emotions (sadness and happiness) Page 9/23

Page 10

are not merely opposite ends of a single continuum but represent two distinct emotional dimensions62,63. This concept is further illustrated by the notion of uncoupled activation, where emotions are independently activated as either positive or negative rather than simultaneously. Perhaps, therefore, in accordance with the "uncoupled activation" hypothesis64, loneliness as a negative emotion intensifies other negative emotions (such as sadness) but does not diminish positive emotions (such as happiness).

Irritable Mood ("Nervous, Tense, or On Edge") Our study also identified a unidirectional effect of loneliness on irritable mood manifesting as feelings of nervousness, tension, or edginess65. Loneliness, as an unpleasant (distressing) emotional condition characterized not only by sadness but also by anxiety66,67, often results in heightened social vigilance and distrust in social situations68, leading to cognitive distortions in interpreting social cues as threats. Then, such distortions can contribute to irritability, which is also recognized as a symptom of depressive mood, as well as anxiety disorders causing social withdrawal69.

Simultaneously, we did not find evidence that irritability leads to increased loneliness. This suggests that the vicious cycle between loneliness and irritability, in which irritability may reduce the likelihood of forming satisfying relationships and increase feelings of isolation, may require more time to develop. Therefore, future research on this relationship should consider longer time frames, as demonstrated by Danneel et al.70.

Keeping Busy Our findings reveal a unidirectional relationship where loneliness leads to increased busyness. Perhaps lonely people tend to seek out activities that provide distraction, offering temporary relief from their discomfort71. Among adolescents, distractions from loneliness include passive screen time (like gaming or watching TV), music, reading, homework, writing, drawing, cycling, and running72.

At the same time, the reason busyness does not contribute to loneliness may lie in the nature of these activities. Although they are primarily solitary behaviors73, they often provide a sense of fulfillment and help individuals feel productive or engaged, preventing emotional distress74. Therefore, while loneliness may prompt individuals to become busier, this increased activity does not deepen feelings of loneliness. In fact, loneliness motivates people to keep busy as a form of distraction, but busyness itself serves as an effective tool for emotional regulation and doesn't lead to increased loneliness. Boredom Our analysis reveals a significant unidirectional link between boredom and increased feelings of loneliness. Previous research suggests that boredom is not merely a result of inactivity; it reflects a deeper psychological discomfort, encompassing sadness, emptiness, anxiety, lack of motivation, and emotional disengagement75,76. It stems from an inability to engage in fulfilling activities, leading to frustration and dissatisfaction. While boredom can initially act as a motivator to seek rewarding Page 10/23

Page 11

experiences77, chronic boredom signals deeper issues with adapting to one’s environment and engaging effectively in daily life78.

Individuals prone to boredom often exhibit lower self-control and may engage in negative or unproductive behaviors. This can result in feelings of disconnection from their surroundings and a loss of control over their lives75. Such feelings may increase loneliness, as these individuals perceive social interactions as unsatisfying or trivial79, leading to less fulfilling relationships and feelings of rejection80,81.

Interestingly, loneliness did not show a prospective impact on future feelings of boredom. This might be because lonely people often use busyness as a strategy to distract themselves from their loneliness71. These distraction activities (see more in the Keeping Busy subsection), while non-social in nature, can provide a sense of engagement and temporary satisfaction, reducing the likelihood of boredom being experienced.

Parental Pressure Finally, our analysis identified a bidirectional relationship between parental pressure and adolescent loneliness. This creates a cycle where early parental pressure increases loneliness, and loneliness, in turn, amplifies the perception of that pressure. This dynamic aligns with the interpersonal model of youth depression, which suggests that disruptions in family relationships–particularly those stemming from insecure attachments or excessive parental expectations–increase the risk of depression82. Excessive parental expectations regarding academics, extracurricular activities, or social behavior can make adolescents feel misunderstood, leading them to withdraw from family interactions and experience lowered self-esteem83,84.

Low self-esteem plays a crucial role in this cycle. Adolescents with diminished self-worth often struggle with feelings of rejection and isolation, not only within their families but also in peer interactions85. This sense of rejection may lead to maladaptive behaviors, such as avoiding emotional openness and exhibiting colder attitudes, which further strain relationships86. As a result, strained family relationships and limited peer interactions intensify feelings of loneliness.

At the same time, the cyclical nature of the relationship between parental pressure and loneliness indicates that not only can parental pressure lead to increased loneliness, but loneliness can also heighten perceptions of parental pressure. This is because one lonely adolescents are more sensitive to parental guidance, interpreting it as excessive pressure–a phenomenon of "increased sensitivity". Parents may respond to their child's withdrawal by more control, which the adolescent interprets as additional pressure–a phenomenon of "parental reaction to withdrawal"87. This feedback loop deepens both parental pressure and loneliness, significantly affecting adolescents' emotional well-being.

Practical Implications Page 11/23

Page 12

Our analyses confirm relationships between loneliness and depressive symptoms. These dependencies are crucial for understanding the effectiveness of therapeutic interventions. To effectively support the mental health of children and adolescents, a holistic approach is needed–one that focuses not only on reducing negative emotions but also on promoting positive experiences and enhancing social and emotional skills.

Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) can simultaneously address sadness and loneliness by improving social skills and coping strategies, thus reducing sadness associated with loneliness88. Well-being programs utilizing mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR) and positive psychology techniques can foster happiness and resilience, effectively reducing loneliness89. Group therapies, such as Social Skills Training (SST)90, Dialectical Behavior Therapy (DBT)91, or Interpersonal Therapy (IPT)92, provide a supportive environment for improving social interactions, which can alleviate anxiety related to loneliness.

Parenting workshops, such as the P-Positive Parenting Program, which emphasize effective communication and empathetic parenting, can help balance parental expectations with support, reducing children's feelings of pressure and consequently their loneliness93. These workshops, by promoting compassion and understanding, contribute to creating a more supportive home environment.

Another effective strategy is engaging children and adolescents in organized activities such as hobby clubs, community projects, sports, or volunteering. These activities not only combat chronic boredom but also foster meaningful social connections, providing opportunities for valuable interactions and relationships. Research shows that structured use of leisure time, such as learning a new hobby, effectively reduces feelings of loneliness in children and adolescents94. Limitations This study on the longitudinal interplay between loneliness and depressive feelings in children and adolescents, has some limitations. First, the study is constrained by a correlational research model (nonexperimental design) based on self-reported data at only two time points, limiting our ability to establish causality and fully capture the dynamics of the observed relationships. Secondly, the study's use of a single self-rated item ("How often do you feel lonely?") to assess loneliness may result in under-reporting due to social stigma95. The use of alternative measures, such as multi-item scales (e.g. UCLA or DJGLS)96, could provide a more nuanced understanding of this complex emotion. Third, our study is based on a non-standard measure of depressive symptoms. Subsequent studies should use measures consistent with the DSM classification, such as the General Health Questionnaire-9 modified for adolescents97.

Furthermore, a notable limitation of this study is its focus on children aged 10–12 years. Given the significant psychosocial changes that occur at different stages of development, caution should be exercised in generalizing these findings to other age groups. Another limitation is that the data come from the turn of the century. Since then, research has indicated an increase in both depression and Page 12/23

Page 13

loneliness98–100, linked to the dynamic development of digital communication platforms that have altered the ways younger generations establish and maintain social relationships101. At the same time, these changes do not necessarily mean that the observed relationships between loneliness and depressive symptoms differ significantly from those seen today–their fundamental nature may still hold true. The presented analyses can also serve as a

reference point for examining changes in the

relationship between loneliness and depressive symptoms, allowing for an assessment of how stable these associations are over time and what aspects may shift.

Conclusions In summary, our research confirms the existence of several key relationships between depressive emotional states and loneliness in children and adolescents. Our analysis reveals a longitudinal, bidirectional relationship in which loneliness may lead to sadness due to a feeling of loss in interpersonal relationships, and sadness, in turn, can increase loneliness by impeding the development of fulfilling relationships or through mechanisms beyond direct social interactions (the "extended sadness" hypothesis). Concurrently, our analyses highlight a significant link between happiness and reduced loneliness, where higher levels of happiness predict lower levels of loneliness. The fact that the relationship between loneliness and sadness is bidirectional, while the relationship between loneliness and happiness is unidirectional (only prior happiness leads to a reduction in subsequent loneliness, but not vice versa) underscores the concept of emotional complexity in which happiness and sadness are not bipolar opposites but exist as distinct emotional dimensions.

Our results also demonstrate that loneliness can cause depression by intensifying symptoms of anxiety such as nervousness and tension. It highlights the importance of emotion regulation in interpersonal relationships, revealing a key link between these mental health issues. The analysis also suggests a bidirectional relationship between parental pressure and adolescent loneliness. This loop may be explained by the "increased sensitivity" hypothesis, where lonely adolescents perceive normal guidance as excessive pressure, and the "parental reaction to withdrawal" hypothesis, where parents intensify their involvement in response to withdrawal, which is interpreted as more pressure.

The findings of our study also suggest a one-way relationship between boredom and increasing loneliness over time. Boredom, characterized by discomfort, emptiness, and a lack of participation in satisfying activities, leads to young people perceiving their environment, including social stimuli, as uninteresting and banal. This often results in negative views of social interactions and a sense of disconnection. Consequently, individuals who are prone to boredom may exhibit less interest in others and engage in unproductive behaviors, exacerbating loneliness. Moreover, our study indicates that loneliness tends to lead to a tendency to keep busy, which may represent an ineffective coping mechanism with boredom and loneliness through behavioral distraction involving primarily passive screen time and other solitary activities (boredom → loneliness → distraction via non-social activities → keeping busy). Our research also suggests that although there is no direct prospective link between Page 13/23

Page 14

loneliness and fatigue, boredom may mediate their longitudinal relationship, which indicates the need to take action to break the fatigue → boredom → loneliness cycle.
