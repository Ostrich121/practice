Abstract: Background: The bidirectional association between depressive symptoms and cognitive decline has been extensively studied in Western populations, yet empirical evidence from non-Western contexts, particularly among Chinese older adults, remains limited. This study aimed to examine the longitudinal and symptom-level dynamics between depressive symptoms and cognitive functioning using a cross-lagged panel network (CLPN) approach.

Methods: Data were drawn from two waves (2015, 2018) of the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS), including 6,824 individuals aged 50 years and above. Cognitive performance was assessed via the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), and depressive symptoms were measured using the 8-item Center for Epidemiological Studies Depression Scale (CESD-8). A CLPN model was estimated to identify temporally predictive pathways and symptom-level interactions, with LASSO regularization ensuring model sparsity. Model stability was evaluated through nonparametric bootstrapping.

Results: Findings revealed a reciprocal relationship between cognitive decline and depressive symptoms over time. Sleep disturbance and lack of motivation emerged as pivotal bridge symptoms linking both domains. Specifically, restless sleep, loneliness, and emotional distress predicted subsequent cognitive impairment. 1.Introduction: As the global population ages, the relationship between cognitive function and depressive symptoms among older adults has emerged as a critical area of research (Forbes et al., 2024; Kuehner, 2017; Shimada et al., 2014). Although this relationship has been well documented in Western populations (Byers & Yaffe, 2011), relatively few studies have addressed it among older adults in China, particularly from a dynamic, bidirectional perspective.

In China, the rapid aging trend has heightened public health concerns about cognitive decline, which spans a continuum from mild cognitive impairment (MCI) to dementia (Bai et al., 2022). Recent data show that around 15% of those aged 60+experience MCI, and 6.5% have dementia (Jia et al., 2018). These figures underscore the urgent need for culturally tailored interventions that target modifiable risk factors (R. Ren et al., 2022).

Depressive symptoms are recognized as a major risk factor for cognitive decline (Kim, 2019). accelerating the transition from MCI to dementia and destabilizing cognitive function over time (Liu et al., 2023).

Conversely, cognitive impairment heightens the risk of depressive symptoms, forming a reinforcing cycle between the two conditions (Formánek et al., 2020). Social determinants—such as er has not been peer reviewed. Electronic copy available at: https://ssrn.com/

isolation, lack of support, and family conflict—further exacerbate both conditions (Kanbay et al., 2023; Zhang et al., 2023).

Although prior research has examined the link between depressive symptoms and cognitive decline, most rely on standardized measures that overlook symptom heterogeneity. Depressive symptoms manifest in diverse forms—from emotional distress to somatic complaints (Fried et al., 2014).—and may differentially affect specific cognitive domains like memory and executive function (Ganguli et al., 2011; Rock et al., 2014).

The widespread use of aggregate symptom scores may obscure symptom-specific pathways and their distinct effects on cognition.

Network analysis has recently gained prominence as a tool for capturing the complex interplay between depressive symptoms and cognitive function (Borsboom, 2017; Borsboom et al., 2018; Zainal & Newman, 2023). Unlike traditional models, it conceptualizes psychopathology as a dynamic system where individual symptoms interact across domains (Epskamp et al., 2018). Cross-lagged panel networks (CLPNs), in particular, incorporate temporal dimensions to assess bidirectional relationships between symptoms and cognitive domains over time (Chavez-Baldini et al., 2022). However, most studies to date remain cross-sectional, limiting understanding of temporal dynamics and causal directionality (Robinaugh et al., 2020).

Additionally, many longitudinal studies have focused on single cognitive domains, overlooking the multidimensional nature of cognitive impairment (Leyhe et al., 2017). To address these gaps, this study adopts a CLPN framework to investigate the bidirectional interactions between depressive symptoms and multiple cognitive domains. This dynamic and multidimensional approach aims to elucidate the complex mechanisms linking cognition and depressive symptoms among Chinese older adults, offering guidance for effective, culturally grounded interventions.

2. Methods 2.1 Participants This study utilized data from the China Health and Retirement Longitudinal Study (CHARLS) (Zhao et al., 2014). This study is based on a nationally representative survey of middle-aged and older adults (≥45 years) in China. We included data from the two most recent waves collected prior to the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic: the 2015 wave (Wave 3, Time Point 1) and the 2018 wave (Wave 4, Time Point 2).

The study sample consisted of participants aged 50 years and older who completed assessments of cognitive function and depressive symptoms in both waves.

A total of 6,824 participants were included in this study after screening, consisting of 2,996 males (43.9%) and 3,828 females (56.1%).

2.2 Measures 2.2.1 Cognitive Function Cognitive function was assessed using the Mini-Mental State Examination (MMSE), which has been adapted for use in CHARLS. The MMSE has demonstrated good reliability and validity in Chinese older adults (Li et al., 2016).

Cognitive function comprises two dimensions: memory function and mental State. Memory Function has a score range of 0–10 and is assessed using a word recall test, including both immediate and delayed recall.

2.2.2 Depressive Symptoms Depressive symptoms were measured using the 8-item version of the Center for Epidemiological Studies Depression Scale (CESD-8), a widely used screening tool for depressive symptoms among older adults, which has demonstrated good reliability and validity in Chinese older adults (Chen & Mui, 2014). The CESD-8 consists of eight negatively oriented items, rated on a 4-point Likert scale (0=rarely or never, 3= most of the time), with total scores ranging from 0 to 24. Higher scores indicate more severe depressive symptoms.

2.3 Statistical Analysis 2.3.1 Descriptive Statistics Means and standard deviations were computed for all study variables. Paired-sample t-tests were conducted to examine changes in cognitive function and depressive symptoms from Time 1 (T1) to Time 2 (T2).

2.3.2 Cross-Lagged Panel Network (CLPN) Analysis To investigate the longitudinal relationship between cognitive function and depressive symptoms, a Cross-Lagged Panel Network (CLPN) analysis was performed using the glmnet package in R(Friedman et al., 2010). To enhance model sparsity, Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (LASSO) regularization was applied based on the value of lambda that gives minimum mean cross-validated error. The estimated network was visualized using the qgraph package in R. Nodes in the network represented the different variables of depressive symptoms and cognitive function, and were divided into two different communties according to their sources. The characteristics of network edges were defined as follows: Blue edges indicate positive relationships, and red edges indicate negative relationships, and edge thickness represents effect size.

2.3.3 Centrality and Bridge Centrality Analysis To identify key variables in the cognitive–depression network, Out Expected Influence (Out-EI) and In Expected Influence (In-EI) were computed to measure the influence between variables. The Out-EI of a given node refers to the sum of the weights of the edges pointing from this node to all other nodes in the network. It reflects the extent and nature of the node’s influence on the entire network. The In-EI of a given node refers to the sum of the weights of the edges pointing to this node from all other nodes in the network. It reflects the extent and nature of the influence exerted on this node by the entire network.

Additionally, Bridge Expected Influence (BEI) was calculated to identify variables that serve as bridges between depressive symptoms and cognitive function(Robinaugh et al., 2016). 2.3.4 Network Auxiliary Tests To ensure the robustness of the network analysis results, stability assessments were conducted. Centrality and bridge centrality stability were assessed via case-dropping bootstrapping (1000 bootstrapped samples), where a correlation stability (CS) coefficient larger than 0.5 indicated sufficient stability of centrality and bridge centralith estimates(Epskamp et al., 2018). Edge accuracy was evaluated using nonparametric bootstrapping (1000 bootstrapped samples) to compute 95% confidence intervals (CIs). The difference tests of Out-EIs, In-EI, BEI, and edges were conducted by bootstrapping (1000 bootstrapped samples, α=0.05).

4.Discussion This study provides an in-depth exploration of the complex relationship between depressive symptoms and cognitive function in older Chinese adults. Using cross-lagged panel network (CLPN) analysis, we identified symptom-level interactions between depression and cognitive decline, revealing their bidirectional influences and highlighting the cognitive domains most affected by depression. The findings not only enhance our understanding of the temporal dynamics between depression and cognition but also offer important insights for targeted interventions.

4.1 Mechanism Analysis: The Bidirectional Effects of Depressive Symptoms and Cognitive Function Our findings confirm a bidirectional relationship between depressive symptoms and cognitive decline, consistent with previous research suggesting that depression can contribute to cognitive impairment, while cognitive decline may also exacerbate depressive symptoms (Kuehner, 2017). Notably, the significant inhibitory effect of depressive symptoms on memory function (Cog1) (CESD8 → Cog1, OR = 0.58) suggests that experiencing daily tasks as overly effortful—reflecting low motivation and behavioral disengagement—can suppress the development or maintenance of memory function. This aligns with the mesolimbic dopamine pathway model of depression(Grahek et al., 2019), which suggests that reduced motivation may hinder memory-related cognitive tasks. Neuroimaging studies further support this by showing that apathy is associated with reduced functional connectivity between the ventral striatum and the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, regions crucial for motivation-driven behaviors and memory consolidation (Krishnan et al., 1997).

Furthermore, the association between“I felt depressed”(CESD7) and a decline in mental State (Cog2) (CESD7 → Cog2, OR = 0.74) suggests that prolonged emotional distress may activate the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis via chronic stress responses, er has not been peer reviewed. 4.2 The Role of Sleep: A Neuroprotective Bridge Between Depression and Cognition Our study highlights sleep disturbance (CESD2,“My sleep was restless”) as a key factor linking depressive symptoms and cognitive decline, particularly affecting mental State (Cog2, OR = 0.15). Sleep disruption is known to impair the brain’s glymphatic system, reducing the clearance of neurotoxic waste such as beta-amyloid and tau proteins (Ju et al., 2014; Xie et al., 2013). Additionally, disruptions in slow-wave sleep have been linked to hippocampal atrophy, particularly in older adults(Winer et al., 2019), which is consistent with our findings that sleep disturbances contribute to cognitive decline.

Given sleep’s transdiagnostic role in both depression and cognition, interventions targeting sleep could yield dual benefits—mitigating depressive symptoms while slowing cognitive decline. For instance, a combination of cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-I) and blue light therapy has been shown to enhance sleep efficiency and slow cognitive deterioration in older adults (Altena et al., 2008). 4.3 Loneliness and Sociocultural Factors: Insights from the Chinese Context Our findings indicate a significant association between loneliness (CESD3) and sleep disturbances (CESD2), revealing sociocultural factors influencing depression and cognitive decline among older Chinese adults.

In China, the growing prevalence of“empty nest”elderly—due to urbanization and labor migration—has intensified loneliness among aging individuals (Wang et al., 2017). Unlike Western conceptions of loneliness, which often focus on social isolation, loneliness in Chinese older adults is closely tied to a perceived sense of abandonment(Donovan et al., 2017).

These cultural distinctions highlight the need for tailored loneliness interventions. For example, virtual companionship programs incorporating traditional filial piety values have been shown to reduce loneliness and improve both mental health and cognitive function in older adults (P. Ren et al., 2022). By addressing loneliness and its impact on sleep and cognition, targeted interventions may help break the vicious cycle between depressive symptoms and cognitive decline.

4.4 Implications for Clinical Practice and Interventions Our findings suggest that interventions for older adults in China should not solely focus on cognitive function but also integrate emotional well-being and sleep quality. The identified bidirectional relationships emphasize the need for interventions that simultaneously address depression and cognitive function. For example, pharmacological treatments targeting the dopamine system and non-pharmacological approaches such as psychotherapy and exercise interventions should be combined with cognitive training. Additionally, sleep interventions, such as CBT-I, should be incorporated into comprehensive treatment plans to enhance both psychological and cognitive health.

The identification of“restless sleep”(CESD2) and“lack of motivation”(CESD8) as key mediators in the depression-cognition network supports the development of symptom-based, personalized intervention strategies. Early-stage interventions targeting sleep disturbances may help prevent cognitive decline. Similarly, enhancing motivation and encouraging daily activity participation could mitigate the adverse effects of apathy on memory and cognitive performance.

Second, the longitudinal design, covering two time points, allows for an exploration of the temporal dynamics between depressive symptoms and cognitive function, enhancing the ability to draw reliable causal inferences. Finally, the study employs advanced cross-lagged panel network analysis (CLPN), which offers a more accurate representation of the complex interactions between depressive symptoms and cognitive function compared to traditional statistical methods. Additionally, controlling for potential confounding variables (such as age, gender, and education level) strengthens the robustness and reliability of the findings.

However, there are also certain limitations. First, although the study uses a nationally representative sample, it relies solely on self-reported measures of depressive symptoms and cognitive function, which may introduce subjective biases and affect the accuracy of the results. Second, despite the longitudinal nature of the study, the relatively short time span of three years may not fully capture the long-term effects of depressive symptoms on cognitive decline. Moreover, the study does not account for genetic factors and underlying biological mechanisms, which could provide additional insight into the relationship between depression and cognitive function. Future research should incorporate genetic, neuroimaging, and biomarker data to further explore the biological basis of these associations.

5.Conclusions In summary, our findings provide robust evidence for a bidirectional relationship between depressive symptoms and cognitive function in Chinese older adults. Utilizing a cross-lagged panel network approach, we identified that specific depressive symptoms—particularly those related to emotional distress, sleep disturbance, and motivational deficits—exert significant predictive effects on cognitive decline. These results highlight the critical role of addressing symptom-level variations in depression to inform targeted interventions. Clinically, the study suggests that integrated treatment strategies combining pharmacological, psychotherapeutic, and sleep-focused interventions may be effective in mitigating cognitive deterioration. Future research should extend these findings by incorporating objective biomarkers and longer follow-up periods to further elucidate the underlying neurobiological mechanisms.
